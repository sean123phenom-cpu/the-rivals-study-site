Introduction

The Rivals (1775) is a comedy of manners by Richard Brinsley Sheridan. Set in Bath, England, the play satirizes romantic idealism, social pretensions, and the culture of duelling.

About the Author

Richard Brinsley Sheridan (1751–1816) was an Irish playwright and politician. He is known for witty dialogue and satirical drama, including The School for Scandal.

Why It Matters

The play introduces the word “malapropism” through Mrs. Malaprop. It remains a classic example of Restoration-style comedy and social satire.

Exam Relevance

Students are often asked to analyze themes of love, deception, social class, and comedy techniques. Character analysis and dramatic irony are also common exam topics.

Themes

Romantic Idealism vs Reality

Explanation: Lydia desires dramatic, poverty-stricken romance, while Jack seeks practical love.

Key Scenes: Act I Scene II; Act III

Key Quote: “I love to be in love.”

Sample Exam Paragraph: Sheridan contrasts Lydia’s romantic fantasies with Jack’s realistic intentions to highlight the absurdity of sentimental fiction.

Deception and Disguise

Explanation: Jack disguises himself as Ensign Beverley to win Lydia’s love.

Key Scenes: Act I; Act V

Key Quote: “My real name is Absolute.”

Sample Exam Paragraph: Through disguise and mistaken identity, Sheridan creates dramatic irony and comedic tension.

Language and Social Pretension

Explanation: Mrs. Malaprop misuses words to appear educated.

Key Scenes: Act I Scene III

Key Quote: “He is the very pineapple of politeness!”

Sample Exam Paragraph: Mrs. Malaprop’s speech exposes the vanity and artificiality of social pretensions.

Characters

Jack Absolute

Role: Male protagonist; suitor to Lydia.

Traits: Clever, practical, strategic.

Key Scenes: Disguise as Beverley; duel scene.

Strengths: Intelligent, adaptable.

Weaknesses: Deceptive.

Sample Exam Answer: Jack’s disguise highlights themes of deception and romantic realism.

Lydia Languish

Role: Romantic heroine.

Traits: Idealistic, dramatic, naive.

Key Scenes: Reading sentimental novels.

Strengths: Passionate.

Weaknesses: Unrealistic expectations.

Sample Exam Answer: Lydia represents the dangers of excessive romantic imagination.

Mrs. Malaprop

Role: Lydia’s aunt and guardian.

Traits: Comical, pretentious.

Key Scenes: Word misuse scenes.

Strengths: Determined guardian.

Weaknesses: Ignorant misuse of language.

Sample Exam Answer: Her malapropisms provide humour and social satire.

Act Summaries

Act I

Introduces Lydia, Mrs. Malaprop, Jack Absolute, and the disguise as Beverley.

Turning Point: Jack maintains his false identity.

Act II

Faulkland’s jealousy and Mrs. Malaprop’s letters deepen misunderstandings.

Act III

Lydia discovers Jack’s true identity and feels betrayed.

Turning Point: Lydia rejects Jack.

Act IV

Duel arrangements create dramatic tension.

Act V

Truths are revealed, misunderstandings resolved, and marriages arranged.

Major Turning Point: Reconciliation and comic resolution.
